\subsection{Cybersecurity Tools and Reporting}

Cybersecurity tools such as intrusion detection systems and vulnerability scanners are essential for identifying and managing security risks. Frameworks such as those described by \cite{scarfone2007guide} provide structured approaches for detecting and responding to threats. However, these systems are primarily designed for technical users and often produce outputs that are difficult to interpret for non-technical stakeholders.

Existing dashboards and reporting tools focus on aggregating and visualizing data, but they frequently lack mechanisms for abstraction and explanation. As a result, users without deep technical expertise may struggle to understand the implications of security findings or to translate them into actionable decisions.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Security Contexts}

Explainable AI has gained significant attention as a means of improving transparency in complex systems. Techniques such as LIME \cite{ribeiro2016should}, SHAP \cite{lundberg2017unified}, and counterfactual explanations \cite{wachter2017counterfactual} have been widely studied and applied in domains such as finance, healthcare, and cybersecurity.

While these approaches provide valuable insights into model behavior, they are often applied at the level of individual predictions rather than at the system level. Furthermore, their integration into operational tools remains limited. Existing work largely focuses on improving model interpretability, rather than embedding explainability into the overall architecture of decision-support systems.

\subsection{Software Architecture for Complex Systems}

Software architecture research emphasizes the importance of structuring systems to manage complexity and support maintainability. Bass et al. \cite{bass2012software} highlight that architectural decisions influence not only system performance but also usability and communication.

However, relatively little work has explored how architectural principles can be used to support explainability and user understanding in cybersecurity systems. While abstraction and layering are well-established concepts, their application to bridging the gap between technical outputs and non-technical users remains underexplored.

\subsection{Human-Centered Security and Decision Support}

Research in usable security and human-centered design has demonstrated that users often misinterpret security information, leading to poor decision-making. Cranor \cite{cranor2008framework} argues that security systems must account for human limitations and provide interfaces that support comprehension.

Decision support systems (DSS) literature further emphasizes the need to present information in a way that aligns with user needs and decision contexts. However, existing DSS approaches are not specifically tailored to cybersecurity environments, where data is highly technical and rapidly evolving.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Although prior work has addressed explainability, cybersecurity tooling, and software architecture individually, there is limited research that integrates these perspectives into a unified solution. In particular, there is a lack of software architectures that explicitly incorporate explainability as a core design principle to support non-technical stakeholders in cybersecurity decision-making.

This thesis addresses this gap by designing and evaluating an explainable software architecture that transforms technical cybersecurity outputs into understandable and actionable insights for non-technical users.
